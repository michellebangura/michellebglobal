Equity Metrics in Global AI Deployments

Michelle Bangura · April 2026 · 3 min read

There's a conversation happening in enterprise AI that most vendors are avoiding: what happens to the people the AI was supposed to help when the AI is optimized exclusively for efficiency?

I've built systems across 52 countries and 4 continents. The pattern is consistent: technology deployed without equity metrics optimizes for the population that generates the most revenue, not the population that needs the most help. The algorithm is working correctly — it's just optimizing for the wrong objective.

This is not a social justice argument. This is a unit economics argument.

When an AI system fails to serve a population — whether that's a demographic, a geography, or a role within an organization — it creates a parallel manual process. Someone has to handle the cases the AI can't. That parallel process has a cost: labor, errors, delay, customer dissatisfaction, and the reputational risk of visible inequity.

The enterprise that deploys AI with equity metrics — measuring not just efficiency but distribution of service quality across all populations — eliminates the parallel process. Every case is handled by the system. The edge cases get addressed by design, not by exception.

Here's what equity metrics look like in practice: mean time to resolution segmented by geography, department, and role. Service quality scores segmented by the same dimensions. Ticket deflection rates that are consistent across user populations, not concentrated in the populations easiest to serve.

If your AI deflects 60% of tickets from headquarters but only 15% from regional offices, you haven't deployed AI. You've deployed a tool that serves headquarters and ignores everyone else. The regional offices still have the same manual process they had before — plus the overhead of a system they're supposed to be using but isn't working for them.

The sustainable deployment is the equitable deployment. Not because it's the right thing to do — though it is — but because it's the only deployment model that eliminates the manual exception processes that destroy unit economics at scale.

Build equity metrics into the first sprint, not the last audit. The math is on your side.
